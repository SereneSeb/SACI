### Summary  
A grassroots project on the urban fringe of Dakar that restores ecological housing, food systems, and cultural memory through vernacular design, permaculture, and neighborhood collaboration.

---

### Design Strategy  
- Earthbrick housing oriented for passive cooling and seasonal winds  
- Dry toilets with urine separation and compost cycles  
- Interplanted gardens with indigenous and adapted crops  
- Greywater reuse and rain harvesting systems  
- Neighborhood design charrettes involving all resident groups

---

### Ecological Function  
- Closed-loop sanitation builds soil rather than pollutes  
- Water use drastically reduced through harvesting and reuse  
- Gardens support food security and carbon sequestration  
- Reduces dependency on unreliable state services and imported goods

---

### Community Role  
- Residents trained in ecological construction and permaculture  
- Elders guide vernacular revival while youth lead innovations  
- Women‚Äôs groups manage sanitation and community kitchen cycles  
- Project acts as node of learning for visiting villages and NGOs

---

### Cultural / Symbolic Layer  
- Reclaims soil not only as building material‚Äîbut as ancestral continuity  
- Restores ‚Äúvillage within the city‚Äù as a rhythm of life, not nostalgia  
- Design embodies Senegalese values: mutual aid, rhythm, rootedness  
- Architecture is not an object‚Äîbut a cultural vessel in use

### Healing Design Principles  
- Embodied labor reconnects people to earth, place, and cycle  
- Compost becomes a metaphor of return and regeneration  
- Shade trees, courtyard flows, and food harvests provide quiet rituals of repair  
- Homes feel alive, adaptable, and dignified‚Äîbuilt with care, not extraction

### Outcomes & Impact  
- Dozens of homes built with local materials and knowledge  
- Full neighborhood-scale compost and food system in place  
- Inspiration for similar projects in The Gambia, Mali, and Burkina Faso  
- Reinforced ecological autonomy and community pride

---

- Dry sanitation and permaculture are viable urban infrastructures  
- Local knowledge systems can outpace imported design logics

### Summary  
A regenerative living project in Costa Rica integrating agroforestry, spiritual ecology, and educationâ€”Tierramor weaves design, ritual, and ecology into a bioregional architecture of renewal.

---

### Design Strategy  
- Integrated agroforestry and food forests  
- Bioclimatic and earth-based architecture  
- Water retention landscaping and seasonal flow design  
- Shared ritual spaces: community temples, kitchens, learning hubs  
- Infrastructure as social technology: designed for care and participation

---

### Ecological Function  
- Restores degraded cattle pasture into forest-agriculture mosaic  
- Enhances biodiversity, pollinator corridors, and watershed cycles  
- Closed-loop waste, composting, and nutrient systems  
- Cultivates food, fiber, medicine, and habitat in holistic polycultures

---

### Community Role  
- Stewards land cooperatively through residency and apprenticeship  
- Offers public education in regenerative systems, governance, and embodiment  
- Uses ritual councils and seasonal ceremonies to organize social coherence  
- Models a village-scale response to ecological grief and planetary collapse

---

### Cultural / Symbolic Layer  
- Architecture expresses the story of regenerationâ€”built from reverence  
- Temples, fire circles, and orchards operate as spiritual infrastructure  
- Planning follows lunar, seasonal, and biotic rhythms  
- Invokes land not as resource, but as kin

---

### Healing Design Principles  
- Materials breathe, floors ground, walls curveâ€”regulating emotion  
- Spaces hold silence, laughter, sweat, and rest as shared rituals  
- Water and fire are elemental presences throughout the village  
- Architecture returns the body to slowness, gratitude, and collective presence

---

### Outcomes & Impact  
- Transformed degraded land into a thriving, closed-loop system  
- Educated hundreds of apprentices from around the world  
- Influenced other regenerative villages in Latin America  
- Became a case study in biocultural restoration

---

### Lessons & Transferability  
- True sustainability begins with cultural transformation  
- Ritual and governance are spatial technologies  
- Design must adapt to biome, story, and soul  
- Living systems thrive through pattern, not control

### Summary  
In Kurashiki and other Japanese cities, ornamental fish like koi and medaka thrive in open-air drainage canals. These clean, biologically active waterways reflect a sophisticated water management strategy that separates stormwater and sewage, allowing for ecological function within urban space.

---

### Design Strategy  
- Stormwater-only drainage system  
- Stone-lined canal beds and banks for flow clarity  
- Overflow zones integrated with gardens and pedestrian paths  
- Routine maintenance embedded in daily life, not outsourced

---

### Ecological Function  
- Habitat for fish, birds, and aquatic plants  
- Natural mosquito control and waste filtration  
- Microclimate moderation through evaporative cooling  
- Continuous flow prevents stagnation and eutrophication

---

### Community Role  
- Residents inspect, clean, and tend to the waterways  
- Children feed fish, elders teach seasonal rhythms  
- Waterways form neighborhood anchors and paths  
- Collective care reinforces social cohesion

---

### Cultural / Symbolic Layer  
- Koi represent perseverance, peace, and grace under pressure  
- Water is sacredâ€”a mirror, a flow, a promise of life  
- *Satoyama* values turn infrastructure into ritualized ecology  
- These canals are memory corridors, aesthetic rituals, and shared breath

### Healing Design Principles  
- Sound of running water reduces urban stress and attunes nervous systems  
- Visual presence of fish and reflection engages multisensory regulation  
- Infrastructure is touchable, readable, intimateâ€”not anonymous  
- Walking by the canal is a civic meditation

### Outcomes & Impact  
- Model for visible, low-tech ecological infrastructure  
- Enhanced biodiversity and seasonal awareness in urban zones  
- High community satisfaction and engagement  
- Inspiration for city-level water separation strategies worldwide

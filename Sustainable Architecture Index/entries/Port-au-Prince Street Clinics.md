### Summary  
Street-side pop-up clinics in Haiti responding to systemic collapseâ€”built from salvaged materials and run by neighborhood coalitions and diaspora-supported volunteer networks.

---

### Design Strategy  
- Constructed from necessity using scavenged materials and local ingenuity  
- Prioritizes mobility, shelter, and visibility  
- Low-tech and adaptable: folding tables become operating surfaces, tarps become roofs  
- Frequently relocated or rebuilt due to weather or conflict  

---

### Ecological Function  
- Minimal material and energy use through salvage and reuse  
- Avoids permanent ground disturbance  
- Situated in open-air environments to reduce infection spread  
- Sometimes integrated with edible or medicinal plantings  

---

### Community Role  
- Offer healthcare access where institutions no longer function  
- Strengthen networks of care, trust, and solidarity  
- Operate through local initiative, not external command  
- Train youth and distribute herbal, emotional, and first-aid knowledge  

---

### Cultural / Symbolic Layer  
- Represent resilience under abandonment  
- Embody shared refusal to disappear  
- Hold symbolic power as public spaces of touch, voice, and dignity  
- Sites where care becomes visibility, not secrecy  

---

### Healing Design Principles  
- Emotional regulation through constancy, recognition, and communal space  
- Dignity restored through attention, listening, and small physical comforts  
- Healing enacted relationally: not just medical, but affective  
- The clinic as both wound and repair

---

### Outcomes & Impact  
- Saved lives with minimal infrastructure  
- Increased neighborhood autonomy and coordination  
- Reaffirmed culture of [[*konbit*]] (collective labor) and mutual aid  
- Emerged as spaces of civic leadership and adaptive hope  

---

### Lessons & Transferability  
- Architecture must be able to improvise when systems collapse  
- Healing requires presence, not perfection  
- The commons is not a buildingâ€”it is a behavior  
- Urban resilience is held in hands, not permits

### Summary  
Yurt settlements are circular, mobile dwellings adapted to the steppe climates of Central Asia, embodying ancestral design wisdom for seasonal and ecological living.

---

### Design Strategy  
- Circular frame for spatial integrity and wind resistance  
- Central smoke hole and hearth as architectural focus  
- Modular structure collapses for transport  
- Internal zoning reflects family, ritual, and seasonal uses

---

### Ecological Function  
- Fully biodegradable materials  
- No foundation or ground disturbance  
- High insulation from felt and layered wall system  
- Suited for extreme temperature variation

---

### Community Role  
- Houses intergenerational households  
- Used in both permanent encampments and migratory cycles  
- Enables seasonal herding and mobility-based subsistence  
- Forms social clusters without formal planning

---

### Cultural / Symbolic Layer  
- Circularity reflects harmony, cosmology, and balance  
- Center-pole and hearth as axis mundi in spiritual practice  
- Architectural expression of movement and return  
- Rehearses generational knowledge in spatial form

---

### Healing Design Principles  
- Warm, insulating materials buffer harsh climates  
- Proportions nurture social cohesion and rest  
- Breathable walls regulate humidity and air quality  
- Space for storytelling, craft, and embodied memory

---

### Outcomes & Impact  
- Continued use in Mongolia and Central Asia despite modernization  
- Inspires contemporary circular dwellings and nomadic design research  
- Maintained as cultural patrimony and ecological model  
- Reimagined in education, tourism, and eco-design settings

---

### Lessons & Transferability  
- Movement is not the opposite of rootedness  
- Vernacular form can guide contemporary adaptation  
- Architecture can rehearse memory, not just space  
- Seasonal housing fosters ecological attunement

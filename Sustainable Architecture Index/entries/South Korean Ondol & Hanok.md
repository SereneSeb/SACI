### Design Strategy  
- Radiant underfloor heating using wood-fired flue systems (ondol)  
- Clay plaster walls, timber joinery, and modular layouts  
- Passive solar gain through orientation  
- Shared courtyards and layered thresholds  

**Craftsmanship & Materiality**  
Hanok carpentry and masonry use timber without nails, clay tile roofing, and earth floors. Ondol flue construction requires artisan expertise tailored to household scale.

---

### Ecological Function  
- Low-energy heating system reduces fossil fuel dependence  
- Breathable wall systems regulate humidity and indoor air  
- Courtyard ecology supports passive cooling and seasonal shade  
- Use of local materials reduces embodied energy

---

### Community Role  
- Preservation initiatives led by local families and cultural ministries  
- Touristic and educational centers managed as commons  
- Craftsmanship passed through apprenticeship and social networks  
- Seasonal rituals engage collective maintenance (e.g., chimney clearing, papering walls)

---

### Cultural / Symbolic Layer  
- Fire beneath the body as a source of rest, vitality, and grounding  
- Hanok forms reflect Confucian harmony and social hierarchy  
- Architecture tied to agricultural calendars and family rituals

### Healing Design Principles  
- Radiant heat supports circulation and restful sleep  
- Sitting, eating, and sleeping on heated floors fosters intimacy and relational bonding  
- Spatial alignment with seasons and landscape cultivates emotional stability  
- Courtyards support nature immersion and psychological relief

---

### Outcomes & Impact  
- Revalorization of traditional housing as viable, desirable, and sustainable  
- Community renewal through pride in heritage craftsmanship  
- Enhanced public understanding of thermal comfort beyond mechanical systems
